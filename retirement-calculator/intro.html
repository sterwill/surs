Illinois Pension Reform – Some Sample Numbers

For several years Illinois politicians and newspaper editorial pages have complained regularly and loudly about the State's "pension problem". Pensions for State workers have been characterized as "lucrative" and "unsustainable". The impression has been left that Illinois workers' pay and pension benefits are the cause of the State's dire fiscal problems. They also propose the way to solve these problems is to "reform" – reduce these benefits. Largely ignored in these complaints is that the State has not paid its required share of worker pension benefits for decades. A more accurate description of the current situation is that Illinois has a huge debt problem and may, or may not, have a "pension problem." The debt problem has been calculated at about $80 billion. Less factual information has been reported about the "pension problem."

In order to take a closer look at the "pension problem", the following pages will afford you the opportunity to see sample numbers (mathematical facts) involved in one of the State's several retirement systems. The following model emulates the State Universities Retirement System's Traditional, Tier I plan. Presumably, this is one of the plans causing Illinois’ fiscal problems. This plan, which includes the majority of SURS employees, has already been "reformed" to lower benefits for all future workers.

Keep in mind that Illinois law excludes all SURS workers from the Nation's "standard retirement plan" – Social Security. Illinois has chosen to exclude many of its workers from Social Security on the assumption that the State's alternative plan will provide worker benefits at least equal to those provided under Social Security. Since the SURS plan requires workers to "contribute" 8% of their gross earnings into their retirement plan (more than they would pay under Social Security) it would seem logical that the State would also "contribute" an amount at least equal to the Social Security "employer share" of 6.2% of gross earnings. Unfortunately, unlike Social Security, there is no set amount the State must pay into its employee retirement plan, only that the State must pay all retirement annuities when they are due.

The following model is the sole responsibility of John Terwilliger (a SURS retiree) and has not been approved by SURS. Try it out and decide for yourself what is a fair and sustainable retirement plan. Reform may be as simple as the State making regular, required payments into the system.
